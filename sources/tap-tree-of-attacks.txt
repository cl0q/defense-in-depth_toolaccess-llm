# Tree of Attacks with Pruning (TAP)

## Metadata
* **Title:** Tree of Attacks: Jailbreaking Black-Box LLMs Automatically
* **Authors:** Anay Mehrotra, Manolis Zampetakis, Paul Kassianik, Blaine Nelson, Hyrum Anderson, Yaron Singer, Amin Karbasi (Yale University, Robust Intelligence, Google Research)
* **Date/Version:** arXiv:2312.02119v3 (Oct 2024)
* **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.02119

---

## 1. Abstract
While Large Language Models (LLMs) display versatile functionality, they continue to be vulnerable to jailbreak attacks. 
This paper presents **Tree of Attacks with Pruning (TAP)**, an automated, black-box method for generating jailbreaks that produce natural-language prompts. TAP utilizes an attacker LLM to iteratively refine candidate prompts until one of the refined prompts successfully jailbreaks the target model. 
To ensure query efficiency, TAP incorporates an evaluator LLM that assesses candidate prompts and prunes branches that are unlikely to result in a successful jailbreak before sending them to the target.
In empirical evaluations, TAP jailbreaks state-of-the-art LLMs (including GPT-4-Turbo and GPT-4o) with a success rate of over 80% (up to 94% on GPT-4o), outperforming previous methods while using significantly fewer queries. It is also capable of bypassing models protected by guardrails such as Llama Guard.

---

## 2. Methodology
TAP uses a tree-search approach to optimize prompts. The attack is guided by two auxiliary models: an **Attacker LLM** (generates variations) and an **Evaluator LLM** (assesses quality and decides pruning).

### TAP Attack Cycle
1. **Initialize:** Start with the target task (e.g., "Write a phishing email") as the root of the search tree.
2. **Branching:** The Attacker LLM generates $W$ (width) variations of the active prompts, trying different phrasing, contextual templates, or roleplays.
3. **Pruning (Phase 1):** The Evaluator LLM reviews the generated variations *before* they are sent to the target. If a prompt lacks coherence, does not address the topic, or is highly likely to be rejected, it is pruned from the tree. This saves significant queries to the target LLM.
4. **Attack & Assess:** Query the target LLM with the remaining prompts. The Evaluator LLM reviews the target's output and scores it (on a scale of 1-10) based on how well the target fulfilled the harmful request.
5. **Goal Verification:** If the score is 10 (successful jailbreak), the attack completes and returns the prompt.
6. **Pruning (Phase 2):** If no jailbreak is found, TAP retains only the top $K$ highest-scoring prompts to build the next level of the tree, pruning the rest.

## 3. Results & Key Findings
* **State-of-the-Art Success:** On the AdvBench subset dataset:
  * **GPT-4o:** TAP achieves a **94% success rate** (compared to 78% for the previous state-of-the-art).
  * **GPT-4-Turbo:** TAP achieves a **90% success rate** (using an average of 28.8 queries, compared to 60% for PAIR which used 37.7 queries).
  * **GPT-3.5-Turbo:** TAP achieves a **100% success rate** (using 14.8 queries on average).
  * **Gemini-Pro:** TAP achieves an **84% success rate** (using 27.6 queries).
* **Query Efficiency:** TAP reduces the number of queries sent to the target LLM by **60%** compared to other black-box optimization attacks (like PAIR) because the evaluator LLM filters out low-probability attacks before querying the target.
* **Bypassing Guardrails:** TAP was tested against LLMs shielded by Llama Guard (which acts as an input/output safety classifier). TAP successfully bypassed Llama Guard with a high success rate (e.g., **76% success rate** on Llama-2-70b-Chat protected by Llama Guard).
* **Transferability:** Prompts generated by TAP to jailbreak one model also show moderate transferability (success when sent to another model without modification).
